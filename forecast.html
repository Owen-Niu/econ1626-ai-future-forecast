01 · PRODUCTIVITY

The Automation Dividend

Between 25% and 46% of current work activities in Australia could be automated by 2030. AI-driven productivity gains are projected to add A$80 billion annually — the largest component of the total economic impact. Industries with the greatest opportunity include professional and administrative services (A$27 billion), healthcare and social assistance (A$20 billion), and education and training (A$16 billion).

02 · EMPLOYMENT

Jobs in Transition

Between 3.5 million and 6.5 million full-time equivalent positions could be affected by automation, with 1.8 million to 5.0 million workers needing to change professions. The transition will not be uniform: disruption ranges from 16% of jobs in education to 33% in transport. Regional variation is significant — from 21% in city centres to over 30% in mining regions like the Pilbara. Critically, new jobs will emerge from higher productivity, rising consumer incomes, and greater health spending on an ageing population.

03 · INDUSTRY STRUCTURE

The Great Realignment

Australia's economy will see a profound shift in its industrial composition. Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) — companies with 200 employees or less — are expected to drive 55% of the GDP growth from AI adoption, yet they face the steepest barriers: skills gaps, capital constraints, and limited access to compute infrastructure. Meanwhile, the 'new AI economy' — AI products, services, data centres, and AI-specific chips — could contribute A$20–25 billion in GDP by 2034. The risk is a two-speed economy where large corporations capture most of the gains.
